I'm trying a new approach for this web page: a Single Page Application (SPA).
Basically, all the content is in chunks of HTML, and all media is linked, so no separate files need to go with the HTML file.
When you use the nav buttons, the existing content is hidden and the new content (inside its own div) is loaded.
The effect is a site with multiple pages, but there's just one file! Super easy to share.

Science Museum

Photogrammetry!
Been really interested in this technique lately, where you take lots of pictures of a real-world object from different overlapping angles, then use software (I'm using RealityScan by Epic, free) to stitch them together and create a 3D model. Note that this differs from 3D scanning with LIDAR or depth cameras, which is better for determining space in larger areas like rooms or buildings (but not as good for texture detail).

I'm planning to write up the workflow so others can try it too---capture sculpture, props, costumes, etc and use the models in game engines if you want, put them in your portfolio, etc. The results are really impressive (amazing detail and textures) and the process doesn't take long.

On the trip I found a dozen or so interesting objects to try this on. Here's a music room in a giant "Fairy Castle" exhibit. This was only a few pictures and it was through glass, so it wasn't ideal, but the model is still pretty amazing, check out the video!

Science Museum

VR experience: Cosmic Coaster...not for the faint of stomach!

I didn't try this here, but I did try a similar roller coaster VR experience at an arcade later in the trip........
and for the first time in VR, I got motion sickness! UGH!

So I spent awhile researching about exactly why it happens, and it's not just "VR makes you sick"...there are a LOT of factors that play in, and these are critical for designers.

The main idea is that motion sickness happens when your brain gets conflicting signals from your eyes, your inner ear (which senses motion), and your proprioceptors (which sense body position).
In VR, your eyes see the virtual world moving but the other two systems don't (you're sitting still, or there's simulated motion in the chair but it doesn't match the visuals well). This conflict can lead to nausea, dizziness, headaches, etc. And because it's fully immersive, the effect is FAR stronger than with traditional screens--no reference point for the mind to "ground" itself.
If you find this happening, immediately close your eyes---the visual sense is by far the strongest, so the disconnect will be much less. Even though the chair might be moving (which is disorienting with eyes closed), the visual disconnect is the main cause. Then stop the ride asap and when you're able, walk around and get fresh air.

Even though I bailed out before it got too bad, it took awhile before I felt recovered. A real eye-opener (even while shutting my eyes to avoid nausea) about how important good design is for user comfort in VR!

To reduce motion sickness in VR experiences, designers can use techniques like:
- Minimize rapid or unnatural movements in the virtual environment
- Use teleportation or "blink" movement instead of smooth locomotion, especially if it has acceleration
- Provide a stable reference frame (like a cockpit or vehicle interior)
- Limit the field of view during fast movements (your peripheral vision is very sensitive to motion)
- Give users more control like movement speed, turning methods, and whether the camera moves on its own (generally not good, ESPECIALLY rotation)
- Ensure high frame rates and low latency to reduce visual lag
